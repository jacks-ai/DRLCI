Exploring drug–gene interactions is essential for advancing personalized medicine,
developing novel therapeutics, and mitigating adverse drug reactions. There are
important causal relationships between drugs and genes that must be captured
to reveal underlying mechanisms. While prior studies have effectively identified
direct interactions, they often fail to account for specific interaction types. Graph
Neural Network (GNN)–based methods have gained popularity but suffer from
limitations such as equal reliance on neighboring nodes, which can neglect distinct
interdependencies and further obscure critical causal links. Additionally, most
existing methods focus solely on observable drug and gene data, while neglecting
the potential impact of exogenous variables on the prediction results.
To address these challenges, we propose a novel representation learning framework enhanced
by causal inference. Our framework integrates local causal information with global
network structure. The local module employs Causal-GCN to model local inter-
actions between nodes, enabling the capture of fine-grained causal relationships
within the data. It also integrates intervening variables with the original fea-
tures through causal message passing, enhancing the model’s ability to uncover
implicit relationships.
This effectively facilitates a comprehensive insight into the
core causal mechanisms that drive drug–gene interaction (DGI) prediction. The
global module, implemented via residual graph convolution blocks, ensuring the
retention of comprehensive feature information and improving the model’s over-
all understanding of the global structure. We validate the proposed framework
on three benchmark datasets, comparing its performance against state-of-the-art
methods. Experimental results demonstrate that our model excels in identifying
action relationships and extracting key features between drug-gene pairs, provid-
ing a novel practical paradigm for enhancing prediction accuracy in drug–gene
interaction tasks.
